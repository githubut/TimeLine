200-300 AD: Imperial Crisis and Religious Change

The Roman Empire faces internal strife and external threats, while Christianity continues to spread despite intense persecution under certain emperors.

Major Events

Crisis of the Third Century (235-284 AD)

The Roman Empire experiences extreme instability with over 50 claimants to the throne in 50 years, civil wars, and invasions from barbarians.

Caracalla's Edict (212 AD)

Emperor Caracalla grants citizenship to all free men in the Empire, a revolutionary move that fundamentally changes the concept of being Roman.

Diocletian Reforms (284-305 AD)

Emperor Diocletian divides the Empire into Eastern and Western halves, establishing the basis for what will become the Byzantine and Western Roman empires.

Persecution of Christians

Emperors Decius and Diocletian launch systematic persecutions of Christians, marking one of the most intense periods of Christian martyrdom.

Han Dynasty Collapses (220 AD)

The Han Dynasty in China ends, leading to the period of division and the gradual spread of Buddhism throughout China.

Sassanid Empire Established (224 AD)

The Persian Sassanid Empire replaces the Parthian Empire, becoming Rome's main rival in the East for the next four centuries.

Religious & Cultural Developments

Christian Church Organization

Despite persecution, Christians organize their church with bishops, councils, and written doctrines. The religion becomes increasingly formalized.

Neoplatonism Flourishes

The philosophical school of Neoplatonism develops under Plotinus, influencing both Christian and later Islamic theology.
